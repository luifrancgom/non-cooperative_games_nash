5  Solutions

We define here solutions, strong solutions, and sub-solutions. A non-cooperative game does not always have a solution, but when it does the solution is unique. Strong solutions are solutions with special properties. Sub-solutions always exist and have many of the properties of solutions, but lack uniqueness.

\(S_i\) will denote a set of mixed strategies of player \(i\) and \(\symscr{S}\) a set of n-tuples of mixed strategies.

Definition 5.1 (Solvability) A game is solvable if its set, \(\symscr{S}\), of equilibrium points satisfies the condition

\[ (\symscr{t}; r_i) \in \symscr{S} \text{ and } \symscr{s} \in \symscr{S} \implies (\symscr{s}; r_i) \in \symscr{S} \text{ for all } i\text{'s}. \tag{5.1}\]

This is called the interchangeability condition. The solution of a solvable game is its set, \(\symscr{S}\), of equilibrium points.

Definition 5.2 (Strong Solvability) A game is strongly solvable if it has a solution, \(\symscr{S}\), such that for all \(i\)’s

\[ \symscr{s} \in \symscr{S} \text{ and } p_i(\symscr{s}; r_i) = p_i(\symscr{s}) \implies (\symscr{s}; r_i) \in \symscr{S} \]

and then \(\symscr{S}\) is called a strong solution.

Definition 5.3 (Equilibrium Strategies) In a solvable game set \(S_i\) be the set of all mixed strategies \(s_i\) such that for some \(\symscr{t}\) the n-tuple \((\symscr{t}; s_i)\) is an equilibrium point. [\(s_i\) is the \(i^{th}\) component of some equilibrium point.] We call \(S_i\) the set of equilibrium strategies of player \(i\).

Definition 5.4 (Sub-solutions) If \(\symscr{S}\) is a subset of the set of equilibrium points of a game and satisfies condition 5.1; and if \(\symscr{S}\) is maximal relative to this property then we call \(\symscr{S}\) a sub-solution.

For any sub-solution \(\symscr{S}\) we define the \(i^{th}\) factor set, \(S_i\), as the set of all \(s_i\)’s such that \(\symscr{S}\) contains \((\symscr{t}; s_i)\) for some \(\symscr{t}\).

Note that a sub-solution, when unique, is a solution; and its factor sets are the sets of equilibrium strategies.

Theorem 5.1 A sub-solution, \(\symscr{S}\), is the set of all n-tuples \((s_1,s_2, \ldots, s_n)\) such that each \(s_i \in S_i\) where \(S_i\) is the \(i^{th}\) factor set of \(\symscr{S}\). Geometrically, \(\symscr{S}\) is the product of its factor sets.

Proof. Consider such an n-tuple \((s_1, \ldots, s_n)\). By definition \(\exists \symscr{t}_1, \symscr{t}_2, \ldots, \symscr{t}_n\) such that for each \(i\) \((\symscr{t}_i; s_i) \in \symscr{S}\). Using the condition 5.1 n-1 times we obtain successively \((\mathcal{t}_1; s_1; s_2) \in \symscr{S}, \ldots, (\symscr{t}_1; s_1; s_2; s_3; \ldots; s_n) \in \symscr{S}\) and the last is simply \((s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n) \in \symscr{S}\), which we needed to show.

Theorem 5.2 The factor sets \(S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_n\) of a sub-solution are closed and convex as subsets of the mixed strategy spaces.

Proof. It suffices to show two things:

  1. if \(s_i\) and \(s_i' \in S_i\) then \(s_i^{*} = \frac{(s_i + s_i')}{2} \in S_i\);
  2. if \(s_i^{\#}\) is a limit point of \(S_i\) then \(s_i^{\#} \in S_i\).

Let \(\symscr{t} \in \symscr{S}\). Then we have \(p_j(\symscr{t};s_i) \geq p_j(\symscr{t}; s_i; r_j)\) and \(p_j(\symscr{t}; s_i) \geq p_j(\symscr{t}; s_i'; r_j)\) for any \(r_j\), by using the criterion of 2.1, pg. 31 for an eq. pt. Adding these inequalities, using the linearity of \(p_j(s_1, \ldots, s_n)\) in \(S_i\), and dividing by \(2\), we get \(p_j(\symscr{t}; s_i^{*}) \geq p_j(\symscr{t}; s_i^{*}; r_j)\) since \(s_i^{*} = \frac{(s_i + s_i')}{2}\). From this we know that \((\symscr{t}; s_i^{*})\) is an eq. pt. for any \(t \in \symscr{S}\). If the set of all such eq. pts. \((\symscr{t}; s_i^{*})\) is added to \(\symscr{S}\) the augmented set clearly satisfies condition 5.1, and since \(\symscr{S}\) was to be maximal it follows that \(s_i^{*} \in S_i\).

To attack (b) note that the n-tuple \((\symscr{t}; s_i^{\#})\), where \(\symscr{t} \in \symscr{S}\) will be a limit point of the set of n-tuples of the form \((\symscr{t}; s_i)\) where \(s_i \in S_i\), since \(s_i^{\#}\) is a limit point of \(S_i\). But this set is a set of eq. pts. and hence any point in its closure is an eq. pt., since the set of all eq. pts. is closed [see pg. 4]. Therefore \((\symscr{t}; s_i^{\#})\) is an eq. pt. and hence \(s_i ^{\#} \in S_i\) from the same argument as for \(s_i^{*}\).

Definition 5.5 (Values) Let \(\symscr{S}\) be the set of equilibrium points of a game. We define

\[ v_i^{+} = \max_{\symscr{s} \in \symscr{S}} [p_i(\symscr{s})] \text{ , } v_i^{-} = \min_{\symscr{s} \in \symscr{S}} [p_i(\symscr{s})] . \]

If \(v_i^{+} = v_i^{-}\) we write \(v_i = v_i^{+} = v_i^{-}\). \(v_i^{+}\) is the upper value to player \(i\) of the game; \(v_i^{-}\) the lower value; and \(v_i\) the value, if it exists.

Values will obviously have to exist if there is but one equilibrium point.

One can define associated values for a sub-solution by restricting \(\symscr{S}\) to the eq. pts. in the sub-solution and then using the same defining equations as above.

A two-person zero-sum game is always solvable in the sense defined above. The sets of equilibrium strategies \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) are simply the sets of “good” strategies. Such a game is not generally strongly solvable; strong solutions exist only when there is a “saddle point” in pure strategies.

Simple examples

These are intended to illustrate the concepts defined in the paper and display special phenomena which occur in the game.

The first player has the roman letter strategies and the pay-off to the left, etc.

Example 5.1 \(\begin{array}{rcrl} 5 & a\alpha & -3 & \text{Weak Solution: } \left( \frac{9}{16}a + \frac{7}{16}b, \frac{7}{17}\alpha + \frac{10}{17}\beta \right) \\ -4 & a\beta & 4 & \\ -5 & b\alpha & \phantom{-1}5 & v_1 = -\frac{5}{17}, v_2 = +\frac{1}{2} \\ \phantom{1}3 & b\beta & -4 & \end{array}\)

Example 5.2 \(\begin{array}{rcrl} 1 & a\alpha & 1 & \text{Strong Solution: } (b, \beta) \\ -10 & a\beta & 10 & \\ 10 & b\alpha & -10 & v_1 = v_2 = -1 \\ -1 & b\beta & 1 & \end{array}\)

Example 5.3 \(\begin{array}{rcrl} 1 & a\alpha & 1 & \text{Unsolvable; equilibrium points } (a, \alpha), \\ -10 & a\beta & -10 & (b, \beta) \text{ and } \left( \frac{a}{2} + \frac{b}{2}, \frac{\alpha}{2} + \frac{\beta}{2} \right). \\ -10 & b\alpha & -10 & \text{The strategies in the last case have} \\ 1 & b\beta & 1 & \text{maxi-min and mini-max properties.} \end{array}\)

Example 5.4 \(\begin{array}{rcrl} 1 & a\alpha & 1 & \text{Strong Solution: all pairs of mixed} \\ 0 & a\beta & 1 & \text{strategies} \\ 1 & b\alpha & \phantom{-1}0 & v_1^{+} = v_2^{+} = 1, v_1^{-} = v_2^{-} = 0 \\ \phantom{-1}0 & b\beta & 0 & \end{array}\)

Example 5.5 \(\begin{array}{rcrl} 1 & a\alpha & 2 & \text{Unsolvable; eq. pts. } (a, \alpha), (b, \beta), \\ -1 & a\beta & -4 & \text{and } \left( \frac{1}{4}a + \frac{3}{4}b, \frac{3}{8}\alpha + \frac{5}{8}\beta \right). \text{ However}, \\ -4 & b\alpha & -1 & \text{empirical tests show a tendency} \\ \phantom{-1}2 & b\beta & \phantom{-1}1 & \text{toward } (a, \alpha). \end{array}\)
